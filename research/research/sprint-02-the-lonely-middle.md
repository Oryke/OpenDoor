# Sprint 02 — Mapping the "Lonely Middle"

## Objective

One idea kept coming up during our first research sprint:

What happens between finishing tutorials and making a first real contribution?

I wanted to understand that journey in more detail before making assumptions about where OpenDoor could help.

Rather than thinking about solutions, this sprint focused on understanding the experience itself.

---

## What I Learned

One thing became clear very quickly.

Learning to code and contributing to open source are often treated as if they're naturally connected.

They're not.

There's a long stretch in between where many learners quietly stop.

Not because they aren't capable.

More often because they lose confidence, don't know what to do next, or aren't sure whether they belong.

For now, I'm referring to this stage as **"the lonely middle."**

It's a working hypothesis, not a conclusion.

---

## The Journey

From the research, the journey looks something like this:

1. Finish learning the basics.
2. Wonder what comes next.
3. Search for a project.
4. Try setting everything up.
5. Find an issue to work on.
6. Figure out how to introduce yourself.
7. Work through the problem alone.
8. Submit a pull request.
9. Wait for feedback.
10. Decide whether to continue.
11. Either return... or quietly disappear.

Looking at it this way made me realise something.

The challenge isn't one big obstacle.

It's a series of small moments where uncertainty slowly builds.

---

## A Pattern I Didn't Expect

Before this sprint, I assumed technical problems would be the biggest barrier.

The research challenged that assumption.

Technical issues certainly matter, but many studies suggest that social and emotional barriers appear just as often, if not more.

Things like:

- wondering if you're "ready"
- worrying about asking a basic question
- not knowing community etiquette
- waiting for feedback
- interpreting silence as rejection
- taking code review personally

These aren't coding problems.

They're human problems.

---

## One Statistic That Stood Out

One finding kept appearing across the research.

Many people who attempt an open-source contribution only try once.

Whether the first experience goes well or badly has a significant impact on whether they ever try again.

That doesn't necessarily mean their first contribution has to succeed.

It may simply mean the experience needs to feel survivable enough that they're willing to come back.

---

## Questions This Raises

This sprint answered some questions, but created even more.

- Is "the lonely middle" experienced by every beginner?
- Which stages are the hardest?
- Do career changers experience this differently from university students?
- What role does geography or language play?
- How many people never even attempt their first contribution?

These are questions I'd like to validate through conversations with real beginners rather than relying only on published research.

---

## What Changed My Thinking

When I started OpenDoor, I thought I was exploring beginner-friendly communities.

I'm beginning to think the bigger question is different.

Maybe the real challenge isn't helping people learn.

Maybe it's helping them make the transition from learning to belonging.

I'm not ready to say that's the answer.

But it's a direction worth continuing to investigate.

---

## Confidence Level

**Medium**

This sprint is supported by published research and existing literature, but still needs validation through conversations with real beginners.

---

## Next Step

The next phase of research is to speak directly with beginners and compare their experiences with the patterns found in the literature.

The goal isn't to prove this hypothesis right.

It's to find out where it's wrong.
